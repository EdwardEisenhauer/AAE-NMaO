\subsection{Problem~2}%
\label{problem:2}

Check the first- and second-order optimality conditions for the quadratic functions:
\begin{tasks}(1)
  \task $f_a(\matr{x}) = 2x_1^2 - x_1 x_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}x_2^2 - 3x_1 + 3.5$,
  \task $f_b(\matr{x}) = -\tfrac{3}{2}x_1^2 + x_1 x_2 - \tfrac{1}{2}x_2^2 + 2x_1 - 1$,
  \task $f_c(\matr{x}) = x_1^2 + 8x_1 x_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}x_2^2 - 10x_1 - 9x_2 + \tfrac{9}{2}$.
\end{tasks}
Draw their contour plots and classify the stationary points.
Are these functions convex? What kind of stationarity do they have?

\subsubsection*{Mathematics}

A general quadratic function on $\mathbb{R}^2$ can be written as
\begin{equation*}
  f(\matr{x}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\matr{x}^T \matr{G}\, \matr{x} + \matr{c}^T \matr{x} + d,
\end{equation*}
where $\matr{G} = \nabla^2 f$ is the (constant) Hessian.
Setting $\nabla f(\matr{x}^*) = \matr{G}\matr{x}^* + \matr{c} = \matr{0}$ gives the
stationary point $\matr{x}^* = -\matr{G}^{-1}\matr{c}$ (when $\matr{G}$ is invertible).

The character of the stationary point is determined by the Hessian eigenvalues, verified
via Sylvester's criterion applied to $\matr{G}$ \citep{Zdunek}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item all $M_k > 0$: $\matr{G}$ positive definite $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{minimizer},
  \item $M_1 < 0$ and $M_2 > 0$:
        $\matr{G}$ negative definite $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{maximizer},
  \item $\det(\matr{G}) < 0$: $\matr{G}$ indefinite $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{saddle point}.
\end{itemize}

A quadratic $f$ is \textbf{convex} if and only if $\matr{G}$ is positive semi-definite
(all eigenvalues $\geq 0$), and \textbf{strictly convex} if $\matr{G}$ is positive definite.
For a strictly convex quadratic the unique stationary point is a \textbf{global minimizer};
for a strictly concave one it is a \textbf{global maximizer}.

\subsubsection*{Solution}

For each function the Hessian is constant; the stationary point solves
$\matr{G}\matr{x}^* = -\matr{c}$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{(a)}
\begin{equation*}
  \matr{G}_a = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
  \matr{c}_a = \begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
  \matr{x}^*_a = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation*}
Leading principal minors: $M_1 = 4 > 0$, $M_2 = \det(\matr{G}_a) = 3 > 0$ ---
$\matr{G}_a$ positive definite $\Rightarrow$ $f_a$ is \textbf{strictly convex} and
$\matr{x}^*_a$ is a \textbf{global minimizer}.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{(b)}
\begin{equation*}
  \matr{G}_b = \begin{bmatrix} -3 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
  \matr{c}_b = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
  \matr{x}^*_b = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation*}
Leading principal minors: $M_1 = -3 < 0$, $M_2 = \det(\matr{G}_b) = 2 > 0$ ---
$\matr{G}_b$ negative definite $\Rightarrow$ $f_b$ is \textbf{strictly concave} (not convex) and
$\matr{x}^*_b$ is a \textbf{global maximizer}.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{(c)}
\begin{equation*}
  \matr{G}_c = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 8 \\ 8 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
  \matr{c}_c = \begin{bmatrix} -10 \\ -9 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
  \matr{x}^*_c = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation*}
Leading principal minors: $M_1 = 2 > 0$, $M_2 = \det(\matr{G}_c) = -62 < 0$ ---
$\matr{G}_c$ indefinite $\Rightarrow$ $f_c$ is \textbf{neither convex nor concave} and
$\matr{x}^*_c$ is a \textbf{saddle point}.
